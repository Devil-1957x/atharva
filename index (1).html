Bhopal Gas Tragedy

Introduction

Date and Location: The Bhopal disaster occurred on December 3, 1984, in Bhopal, Madhya Pradesh, India.

Chemical Leak: Around 45 tons of methyl isocyanate (MIC) gas leaked from an insecticide plant owned by Union Carbide Corporation.

Immediate Impact: The gas spread over densely populated areas, killing thousands of people instantly and causing a mass panic.

Cause Of Disaster

Corporate Negligence: The disaster was partially attributed to decaying facilities, lack of safety culture, and an undertrained workforce, allowing water to enter the MIC tanks.

Underinvestment in Safety: Management and local authorities were criticized for underinvesting in safety measures, which led to inoperable safety systems, such as turning off the MIC tank refrigeration system.

Safety and Maintenance Issues: Overfilling of MIC tanks, poor maintenance, and inoperable safety devices due to cost-cutting and reduced skilled staff were major factors that exacerbated the disaster.

Worker Sabotage Theory: An alternative theory suggests that a rogue employee deliberately introduced water into the MIC tanks, a scenario that some believe was covered up by the Indian government to shift the blame.

Background

UCIL Factory Construction: Built in 1969 to produce the pesticide Sevin, with MIC used as an intermediate.

MIC Production Addition: In 1979, an MIC production plant was added to the site to enhance production efficiency.

Chemical Process: The process involved methylamine reacting with phosgene to produce MIC, which then reacted with 1-naphthol to form carbaryl.

Alternative Methods: Other manufacturers like Bayer produced carbaryl without using MIC, though at higher manufacturing costs.

Excess MIC Accumulation: Despite reduced demand in the early 1980s, unused MIC accumulated at the Bhopal plant, increasing risks.

Lesson Learned

Industrialization Risks: Expanding industrialization in developing countries without evolving safety regulations can lead to catastrophic outcomes, as seen in Bhopal.

Global Market Ties: The Bhopal disaster highlighted that local industrial hazards are often influenced by global market dynamics, such as UCC's pursuit of the growing Indian pesticide market.

Double Standards: The execution of UCC's project in India suggested a double standard in safety practices for multinational corporations operating in developing countries.

Need for International Standards: Enforceable international regulations could improve safety in hazardous industries by providing uniform safety benchmarks for companies worldwide.

Weak Infrastructure: In 1984, Bhopal's weak public health infrastructure and lack of basic services made it ill-equipped to handle industrial accidents, emphasizing the need for strong local systems before hosting hazardous industries.
